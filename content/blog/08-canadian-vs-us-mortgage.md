## Introduction

If you've ever compared notes with a neighbor across the border about mortgages, you've likely discovered that Canadian and American mortgage systems operate on fundamentally different principles. What Americans call "refinancing" and Canadians call "renewal" aren't just different terms for the same thing. They represent distinct approaches to homeownership financing that affect everything from payment structures to long-term planning.

Understanding these differences matters whether you're a Canadian considering your renewal options, an American curious about the Canadian system, or someone who's moved between the two countries and needs to navigate an unfamiliar mortgage landscape.

This guide breaks down the key differences between Canadian mortgage renewals and US refinancing, helping you understand both systems and make informed decisions regardless of which side of the border your home sits on.

## The Fundamental Difference: Term vs. Amortization

The core distinction lies in how each country structures mortgage contracts.

### US Structure: One Loan for the Duration

In the United States, a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage means exactly that: you lock in one interest rate for 30 years. The rate, the payment, and the contract remain constant unless you choose to refinance.

**Example: US $400,000 mortgage**
- 30-year fixed at 6.5%
- Payment: $2,528/month
- Rate locked for 30 years
- No required action unless you want to refinance

### Canadian Structure: Short Terms Within Long Amortizations

In Canada, mortgages separate the term (the length of your rate contract) from the amortization (the total time to pay off the loan).

**Example: Canadian $400,000 mortgage**
- 25-year amortization
- 5-year fixed term at 5.0%
- Payment: $2,338/month
- Must renew or refinance every 5 years

### Why This Matters

The Canadian system requires regular engagement with your mortgage. Every time your term ends, you must either:
1. Renew with your current lender
2. Switch to a new lender
3. Refinance and change your loan structure

Americans only face this decision when they actively choose to refinance. This creates different risk profiles and different strategic considerations.

## Prepayment Penalties: A Major Difference

One of the most significant differences involves what happens if you pay off your mortgage early.

### US Prepayment

For most US mortgages originated after 2014, prepayment penalties are prohibited on Qualified Mortgages. You can pay extra, pay off the loan entirely, or refinance at any time without penalty.

This means:
- Refinance whenever rates drop enough to make it worthwhile
- Make extra payments freely
- Sell your home without penalty concerns

### Canadian Prepayment

Canadian mortgages almost always carry prepayment penalties during the term. Breaking your mortgage early typically costs:

**Fixed-rate mortgages:** The greater of:
- Three months' interest, OR
- Interest rate differential (IRD): the difference between your rate and current rates for the remaining term

**Variable-rate mortgages:**
- Usually three months' interest

### The IRD Trap

The Interest Rate Differential can result in massive penalties, especially when rates have dropped:

**Example:**
- Original mortgage: $400,000 at 5.0% fixed
- 3 years into a 5-year term
- Current rates: 3.5%
- Remaining term: 2 years

IRD calculation:
```
($400,000 × (5.0% - 3.5%) × 2 years) = $12,000
```

Three months' interest: ~$5,000

Penalty = higher of the two = **$12,000**

This can make Canadian refinancing prohibitively expensive mid-term, even when rates drop significantly.

### Strategic Implications

**For Canadians:**
- Plan refinancing around term end dates
- Calculate IRD before considering mid-term switches
- Consider shorter terms if you anticipate rate drops
- Variable-rate mortgages have lower penalties (but rate risk)

**For Americans:**
- Refinance whenever the math works
- No penalty calculations needed
- More flexibility in timing decisions

## The Canadian Stress Test

Since 2018, Canadian mortgage applicants must qualify at a "stress test" rate higher than their actual mortgage rate.

### How It Works

Borrowers must qualify at the higher of:
- The Bank of Canada benchmark rate (currently around 5.25%)
- Their actual mortgage rate plus 2%

**Example:**
- Mortgage rate offered: 4.5%
- Stress test rate: 4.5% + 2% = 6.5%
- Borrower must prove they can afford payments at 6.5%

### Impact on Refinancing/Renewal

**At renewal with the same lender:** Stress test often not required, even if you couldn't pass it today.

**When switching lenders:** Full stress test required, potentially limiting options for borrowers whose income has decreased or home values have dropped.

This creates a scenario where some Canadians are "trapped" with their current lender even if better rates are available elsewhere.

### US Comparison

The United States has no equivalent stress test requirement. Borrowers qualify based on the actual rate they'll pay, not a hypothetical higher rate.

## Renewal vs. Refinancing in Canada

It's important to distinguish between these Canadian options:

### Mortgage Renewal

When your term ends, you renew:
- Same lender or new lender
- Same loan amount (no new borrowing)
- May change rate type (fixed to variable or vice versa)
- May change term length
- Simpler process than refinancing
- No stress test if staying with current lender

### Mortgage Refinancing

Refinancing in Canada means changing the mortgage structure:
- Borrowing additional money (accessing equity)
- Changing amortization period
- Consolidating debts
- Adding or removing borrowers

Canadian refinancing:
- Requires stress test qualification
- May incur prepayment penalties if mid-term
- Maximum 80% loan-to-value
- Requires appraisal
- Full application process

### What Americans Call "Refinancing"

The US term "refinancing" encompasses both Canadian concepts:
- Rate-and-term refinance = similar to Canadian renewal + lender switch
- Cash-out refinance = similar to Canadian refinancing

## Rate Types and Their Implications

### Fixed-Rate Mortgages

| Aspect | Canada | United States |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Available terms | 1-10 years | 10-30 years |
| Most common | 5-year fixed | 30-year fixed |
| Rate stability | During term only | Entire loan |
| Prepayment | Penalties apply | Usually free |

### Variable/Adjustable Rates

| Aspect | Canada | United States |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Name | Variable rate | Adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) |
| Rate basis | Bank of Canada prime | Various indices (SOFR, etc.) |
| Adjustment | Immediate (usually) | Periodic (often annual) |
| Payment change | May stay fixed (extend amortization) or adjust | Adjusts with rate |
| Popularity | ~25% of market | ~5-10% of market |

### Open vs. Closed Mortgages (Canadian Concept)

Canada has an additional distinction:

**Closed mortgages:** Limited prepayment (typically 10-20% per year), penalties for breaking
**Open mortgages:** Can be paid off anytime, but higher rates

The US doesn't have this distinction; most mortgages are functionally "open."

## Government Insurance Programs

Both countries have government-backed insurance for higher-LTV mortgages, but with different structures.

### Canada: CMHC and Others

Mortgages with less than 20% down payment require mortgage default insurance from:
- CMHC (Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation)
- Genworth Canada
- Canada Guaranty

Insurance premium: 0.6% to 4.0% of loan amount (added to mortgage)

### United States: Multiple Programs

- FHA: Federal Housing Administration (lower down payments, more flexible credit)
- VA: Veterans Affairs (zero down for veterans)
- Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac: Government-sponsored enterprises (conventional loans)
- PMI: Private mortgage insurance (can be removed at 20% equity)

### Key Difference

Canadian mortgage insurance is permanent (unless you refinance below 80% LTV). US PMI can be removed once you reach 20% equity.

## Strategic Considerations for Each Market

### Canadian Mortgage Strategy

**1. Plan Around Term End Dates**
Start looking for renewal options 4-6 months before your term ends. Lenders often allow rate holds 120 days in advance.

**2. Consider Shorter Terms When Rates Are High**
If rates are elevated, a 2- or 3-year term lets you renew sooner when rates potentially drop.

**3. Evaluate Variable vs. Fixed Carefully**
Variable rates offer lower penalties and may provide better long-term value historically, but require comfort with rate fluctuation.

**4. Negotiate at Renewal**
Your current lender's first offer is rarely the best. Get competing quotes and negotiate.

**5. Calculate IRD Before Breaking a Mortgage**
Mid-term switches can be expensive. Always calculate the penalty before deciding.

### US Mortgage Strategy

**1. Lock Long When Rates Are Low**
Unlike Canada, you can lock in a good rate for 30 years. When rates are favorable, take advantage.

**2. Refinance When Math Works**
No penalties mean you can refinance whenever your break-even calculation is favorable.

**3. Consider 15-Year Terms for Savings**
Lower rates and faster payoff, with flexibility to refinance if needed.

**4. Shop Multiple Lenders**
Each refinance is a new transaction. Always compare.

**5. Don't Time the Market Perfectly**
If the math works today, act. You can refinance again if rates drop further.

## Cross-Border Considerations

### Americans Moving to Canada

Expect a significant adjustment:
- Get comfortable with 5-year terms
- Understand prepayment penalties
- Prepare for the stress test
- Consider variable rates for flexibility
- Build renewal planning into your financial calendar

### Canadians Moving to the US

Take advantage of the differences:
- Consider longer fixed terms for stability
- Enjoy prepayment freedom
- No stress test means qualification at actual rates
- Shop aggressively; competition is fierce
- Refinance strategically when rates improve

## Practical Comparison: Same Scenario, Different Countries

Let's see how the same financial goal plays out differently:

**Scenario:** Homeowner 3 years into their mortgage wants to access $50,000 in equity

### In Canada

**Current situation:**
- $350,000 balance, 5-year fixed at 5.5%
- 2 years remaining on term
- Home value: $500,000

**Process:**
1. Calculate prepayment penalty (IRD may be substantial)
2. Apply for refinance (stress test required)
3. New appraisal needed
4. Maximum 80% LTV = $400,000 loan
5. New term starts fresh

**Potential costs:**
- IRD penalty: $5,000-$15,000
- Appraisal: $300-$500
- Legal fees: $500-$1,000
- Discharge fee: $300

**Total: $6,000-$17,000**

### In United States

**Current situation:**
- $350,000 balance, 30-year fixed at 5.5%
- Home value: $500,000

**Process:**
1. Apply for cash-out refinance
2. No prepayment penalty
3. Appraisal required
4. New 30-year loan at current rates

**Potential costs:**
- Closing costs: 2-3% of loan = $8,000-$12,000
- No penalty

**Total: $8,000-$12,000**

The US process costs less and has no uncertainty about penalty amounts. The Canadian process could cost significantly more due to IRD.

## Conclusion

Canadian mortgage renewal and US refinancing serve similar goals but operate under very different rules. The Canadian system with its short terms builds in regular decision points but penalizes flexibility mid-term. The American system offers long-term rate stability and prepayment freedom but requires proactive monitoring to capture refinancing opportunities.

Key takeaways:
- Canadians must plan around term end dates; Americans can act anytime
- Canadian prepayment penalties (especially IRD) can be substantial
- The Canadian stress test can limit renewal options
- Both systems require understanding your specific market's rules
- Cross-border movers should prepare for significant system differences

Whether you're navigating a Canadian renewal or an American refinance, understanding the mechanics of your market helps you make better decisions and potentially save thousands over the life of your mortgage.
